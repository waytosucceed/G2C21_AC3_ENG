Sentence, Fragments and Run-on

First, we have the perfect sentence train. It has a beginning (the engine), a middle (the carts), and an end (the caboose). It makes a complete thought. Like this: "The cat sleeps on the bed."

But sometimes, we might see a train that's missing parts. Oops! That's called a fragment. It's like seeing just the middle cars without an engine or caboose. Like: "Because of the rain."

Now, what if we have too many trains stuck together without stops? That's a run-on! It's like a super long train that goes on and on and on. Like: "The cat sleeps on the bed the dog barks loudly the bird sings." Whoa, slow down! We need to break this up.

Definition with examples:

Sentences: These are complete thoughts. They tell us who did what.

  • Example: "The dog barked loudly." This is perfect! We know who (the dog) and what (barked loudly).

Fragments: These are incomplete thoughts. They're missing important parts.

  • Example: "Barked loudly." Oops! Who barked? We need to know more!

Run-ons: These are too many thoughts stuck together without stops.

  • Example: "The dog barked loudly the cat ran away the bird flew to a tree." Whoa! That's too much at once! We need to break it up by adding full stops or commas.
